Theory Notes
📡

1. WiFi Uses Radio Waves

WiFi transmits data as radio waves — the same electromagnetic wave used by FM radio and TV, but at a much higher frequency. Your router converts digital data (0s and 1s) into radio waves, broadcasts them, and your device receives and decodes them. Radio waves travel at the speed of light and can pass through walls — though solid objects reduce signal strength.

📶

2. Two Frequency Bands

⚠️ Critical for ESP32-CAM

2.4 GHz

  • Range up to ~45m indoors
  • Better wall penetration
  • More interference (microwaves, Bluetooth)
  • Slower data speed
✅ ESP32-CAM ONLY supports 2.4 GHz

5 GHz

  • Range up to ~20m indoors
  • Blocked more by walls
  • Less interference
  • Faster data speed
❌ ESP32-CAM cannot use 5 GHz
🌐

3. What Does a Router Do?

A router creates a Local Area Network (LAN) — a private network that devices join to communicate with each other. When your phone sends a motor command to the ESP32-CAM, it travels as radio waves to the router, which forwards it to the car. No internet is required — everything is local. Our project uses a dedicated router with no internet connection.

⚠️

4. What Reduces WiFi Signal?

FactorEffect on the Spy Car
DistanceVideo stream becomes choppy then drops as the car drives further from the router.
Walls & obstaclesMetal benches and concrete walls absorb 2.4 GHz signals significantly.
Too many devices20 ESP32-CAMs on one router share the bandwidth — use QVGA resolution in class.
InterferenceMicrowaves, Bluetooth, and neighbouring WiFi networks all share 2.4 GHz.
Wrong frequencyIf the router only broadcasts 5 GHz, the ESP32-CAM fails after 15 seconds with a slow LED flash.

Theory Lesson 1 of 8

How Microcontrollers Work

CPU, Memory, GPIO, and the ESP32-CAM Architecture

🎯

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Understand how microcontrollers differ from general-purpose computers, and explain how the ESP32-CAM processes inputs and controls outputs.

By the end of this lesson I can:

  • Explain what a microcontroller is and give three examples of everyday uses
  • Describe the role of CPU, RAM, flash storage, and GPIO pins
  • Explain why the ESP32-CAM uses two CPU cores and what each does
  • Define 'embedded system' and apply it to the RC car project
🔬

1. What is a Microcontroller?

A microcontroller is a complete computer system on a single chip — it contains a CPU, RAM, flash storage, and input/output pins all in one package. Unlike a general-purpose computer (laptop/phone), a microcontroller is designed to do ONE specific job, embedded inside a product. Examples: washing machine controller, traffic light timer, TV remote, car ABS sensor.

The ESP32-CAM is a microcontroller with: dual-core CPU (240 MHz), 520 KB SRAM + 4 MB PSRAM, 4 MB flash, OV2640 camera, and a 2.4 GHz WiFi/Bluetooth radio — all on a board the size of a matchbox.

⚙️

2. CPU, RAM, Flash and GPIO

ComponentWhat it doesESP32-CAM value
CPUExecutes program instructionsDual-core Xtensa LX6, 240 MHz
RAM (SRAM)Temporary working memory while running520 KB internal + 4 MB PSRAM
FlashPermanent storage — holds your sketch4 MB
GPIOGeneral Purpose Input/Output pins — connect to hardware16 usable pins (avoid GPIO16)
🧠

3. Dual Core Architecture in the RC Car

The ESP32 has two CPU cores. In the v8 sketch: Core 0 handles the WiFi radio and network stack (background, automatic). Core 1 runs your Arduino code — loop(), pageServer.handleClient(), the watchdog, and the stream server callbacks.

🚨

4. GPIO16 Warning — Forbidden Pin

GPIO16 on the AI Thinker ESP32-CAM is connected to the PSRAM chip select line. If you use it as a motor output pin, you corrupt the PSRAM bus, causing the system to crash or freeze on boot. This is why IN4 in the v8 sketch uses GPIO12, not GPIO16.

Theory Lesson 3 of 8

H-Bridge Motor Theory

Current Direction, Voltage, H-Bridges, and the L298N Driver

🎯

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Explain how an H-bridge circuit controls the direction of a DC motor, and apply H-bridge logic to predict motor behaviour.

By the end of this lesson I can:

  • Describe what happens to current direction when motor wires are swapped
  • Explain how an H-bridge uses four switches to reverse motor direction
  • Complete the H-bridge truth table for L298N IN1–IN4
  • Apply Ohm's Law to calculate voltage and current values
🔄

1. DC Motors and Current Direction

A DC motor spins in the direction of current flow through its coils. Swap the two wires and current reverses — the motor spins the other way. This is all motor direction control is: reversing which wire is positive and which is negative.

🔀

2. The H-Bridge Circuit

An H-bridge uses four electronic switches (transistors) arranged in an H shape with the motor in the middle. By closing different pairs of switches you can push current through the motor in either direction — without ever touching the wires.

IN1IN2Motor A
HIGHLOWForward
LOWHIGHBackward
LOWLOWCoast (free)
HIGHHIGHBrake (stop)
⚡

3. Ohm's Law & Voltage in Our Circuit

Ohm's Law: V = I × R  |  I = V ÷ R  |  R = V ÷ I

Our battery is 9V. The L298N has an internal voltage drop of ~2V. So the motors receive approximately 7V — within the TT motor's 3–6V rating (slightly over, but acceptable for short bursts at this scale). The L298N can supply up to 2A per channel.

🔌

4. ENA / ENB Jumpers

The L298N has ENA and ENB enable pins. When the yellow jumper cap is installed, these pins are tied to 5V — permanently enabling both motor channels at full power. This is why the v8 sketch needs no PWM speed control. Remove the jumper and motors stop regardless of IN1–IN4.

💻

2. setup() and loop()

setup() runs ONCE when the board powers on. It initialises pins, starts WiFi, initialises the camera, and starts both web servers. Think of it as setting up a kitchen before you start cooking.

loop() runs repeatedly, forever. In v8 it calls pageServer.handleClient() to process incoming HTTP requests and checks the motor watchdog. Think of it as the ongoing cooking process.

👁️

3. The Motor Watchdog

The v8 sketch has a safety watchdog: if no motor command is received for 1500 ms (1.5 seconds), motorStop() is called automatically. This prevents the car from driving away if the WiFi connection drops. The variable lastCmdTime stores when the last /cmd was received.

🌐

4. URL Handlers and Lambda Functions

pageServer.on("/cmd", []() { ... }); registers a URL route. When the browser requests /cmd, the code inside []() { } runs. The [](){} syntax is a lambda function — an anonymous function defined inline. The query parameter go is read with pageServer.arg("go").

Systems Thinking and Mechatronics

Inputs, Processes, Outputs, Feedback, and the Engineering Design Process

🎯

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Apply systems thinking to analyse the RC car as an IPO system, and explain the engineering design process used to develop it.

By the end of this lesson I can:

  • Define mechatronics and identify its four core disciplines

⚙️

1. What is Mechatronics?

Mechatronics combines four disciplines: Electronics (circuits, sensors, actuators), Mechanical engineering (chassis, motors, gears), Computing (microcontrollers, code, algorithms), and Control systems (feedback loops, automation). The RC car uses all four.

📊

2. Input–Process–Output (IPO)

3. Open vs Closed Loop Control

An open-loop system executes commands without checking if the result was achieved. A closed-loop system measures its output and adjusts. The RC car's driving system is open-loop — it turns the motors but has no sensor to check if the car actually moved.

The motor watchdog is a partial feedback loop: it monitors time since the last command (input), compares to a threshold (comparator), and stops motors if too long has passed (actuator output).

Theory Lesson 6 of 8

Camera Technology and Image Compression

Pixels, JPEG, MJPEG, Resolution, and How the ESP32-CAM Captures Video

🎯

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Explain how digital cameras capture images, describe JPEG compression, and explain how MJPEG streaming works in the RC car.

By the end of this lesson I can:

  • Explain what a pixel is and how resolution is measured (VGA = 640×480)
  • Describe how JPEG compression reduces file size and the quality trade-off
  • Explain how MJPEG streaming works using multipart/x-mixed-replace
  • Explain why PSRAM is needed for higher resolutions on the ESP32-CAM
📷

1. Pixels, Resolution, and the OV2640

ResolutionDimensionsTotal pixelsUncompressed size (8-bit)
QQVGA160×12019,200~19 KB
QVGA320×24076,800~75 KB
VGA640×480307,200~300 KB

The ESP32-CAM uses the OV2640 sensor (2 megapixels max). For streaming we use much lower resolutions — QVGA is the classroom standard as it balances quality and network load.

🗜️

2. JPEG Compression

JPEG is a lossy compression format — it discards some image data to reduce file size. In v8, JPEG quality is set to 12 (scale 0–63, lower = higher quality = larger file). A VGA JPEG at quality 12 is typically 20–40 KB — about 10× smaller than uncompressed. The trade-off: slight blurring and "artifacts" on sharp edges.

🎬

3. MJPEG Streaming Protocol

MJPEG (Motion JPEG) is a video stream of individual JPEG frames sent over HTTP. The v8 sketch uses multipart/x-mixed-replace — the browser opens a single HTTP connection and the server keeps sending new JPEG frames separated by boundary markers. Each new frame "replaces" the previous one in the browser's img tag. No plugins needed — works in any browser.

💾

4. Why PSRAM is Needed

VGA frames are ~300 KB uncompressed. The ESP32-CAM's internal SRAM is only 520 KB — barely enough for one frame plus the rest of the program. The external 4 MB PSRAM allows 2–3 frame buffers (fb_count=2 or 3), so the camera can capture the next frame while the current one is being sent. Without PSRAM, v8 falls back to QVGA with fb_count=1.

Theory Lesson 7 of 8

Cybersecurity and the Internet of Things

IoT Vulnerabilities, Network Security, Passwords, and Safe Coding Practices

🎯

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Identify cybersecurity risks in IoT devices like the RC car, explain attack methods, and evaluate safe practices for connected embedded systems.

By the end of this lesson I can:

  • Define IoT and identify common cybersecurity risks in connected devices
  • Explain what a hardcoded password risk is and why the v8 sketch warns against it
  • Describe three strategies for securing a WiFi-connected embedded device
  • Evaluate the RC car's cybersecurity posture and suggest improvements
🔐

1. IoT and Cybersecurity Risks

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to physical devices with network connectivity — smart TVs, security cameras, thermostats, medical devices, and our RC car. IoT devices are often poorly secured because they are cheap, low-power, and rarely updated. Common risks: default passwords, no encryption, no authentication, publicly exposed ports.

⚠️

2. Hardcoded Credentials — The #1 Risk

The v8 sketch has a clear warning: NEVER submit or share code with your real password. If code with a real WiFi password is uploaded to GitHub, anyone who finds it can connect to that network. This is called a hardcoded credential — a secret embedded directly in source code. Before sharing any code, replace real passwords with "REMOVED".

🛡️

3. How Our Project's Setup Improves Security

The RC car uses a dedicated classroom router with no internet connection. This network isolation means the car cannot reach the school network, the internet, or any other system. Even if someone gained control of the car, they could not use it to access other systems. This is a security principle called network segmentation.

🔍

4. Authentication vs Encryption

ConceptMeaningRC Car Status
AuthenticationProving who you are before access❌ None — anyone on the network can control the car
EncryptionScrambling data so others cannot read it❌ HTTP only, no HTTPS — commands visible on network
Network isolationLimiting what the device can reach✅ Dedicated classroom router, no internet
